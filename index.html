Where is this really coming from?

A quick tool for practitioners and community members to locate where pressure is being produced — before deciding what to do about it.

Understanding the system

Decisions happen at different levels.

When something is not working, it helps to know which level it is coming from. Tap each level to learn more.

Micro — practitioner / client level

You & your worker

This is the conversation between you and the person supporting you. Your worker genuinely wants to help — and this is the level they can act on directly.

If something is not working here, your worker can usually change it. This is where your voice has the most direct effect.

How you are supportedWhat happens in sessionsHow often you connectWhat goals you work toward

Meso — organisation / service level

The organisation & service

This is the service or organisation your worker is part of. They set rules about how services are delivered, who qualifies, and how much time workers have.

Your worker does not always control these rules — but the organisation can change them, especially when communities speak up consistently over time.

Eligibility criteriaWait timesWhat services are offeredHow feedback is handled

Meso / Macro boundary — funding / contract level

Funding & contracts

Services are paid for through funding contracts. These contracts decide what services can be offered, to whom, and for how long. When funding ends or changes direction, services can disappear even when the community still needs them.

Neither your worker nor the organisation always controls this. Community feedback and documented need can influence future funding decisions, even if it takes time.

Program durationWho is eligibleWhen a service gets cutWhat gets measured

Macro — policy / legislation level

Policy & legislation

Laws and policies set the rules that every level below must follow. Even when a worker, service, and funder all agree something is needed, policy can make it impossible to deliver — and the people at those levels are often frustrated by that too.

This is the hardest level to change. But your stories are part of what builds the evidence that shifts policy over time. That matters even when the change feels slow.

Housing legislationChild protection lawImmigration rulesIncome support conditions

What this means for you

When a service cannot help you with something, it does not always mean they do not care or are not trying. Often the limit sits at Level 3 or 4 — above them, and above your worker too.

Knowing which level a problem is coming from helps you know where to direct your energy, and helps your community worker be honest with you about what they can actually change.

Practitioner tool

Tier Check — before the conversation

Use this before supervision, a team debrief, or a community engagement to locate where a pressure is being produced. Takes about two minutes.

1

Name the situation

In a sentence or two, describe what is feeling stuck, heavy, or unclear. Just enough to anchor the check.

2

Where is it being produced?

Where is this pressure most likely being generated? Select the most accurate level.

3

What can you genuinely influence?

At your level, what levers do you actually have? Select all that apply.

The quality of the relationship and how the person feels supported
How I prepare for and use supervision
Documenting patterns and feeding them back to the organisation
Contributing community voices to advocacy or co-design processes
Naming the tier honestly with the person or community I am supporting
4

Where does the next conversation belong?

Who needs to be part of it, and at what level?
